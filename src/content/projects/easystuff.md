# Music Production

This is your Production Get-In started on the 17th of January 2026 by Stella-Lou Pique.

Notes:

You don't have to spend a single dime to get started with music production, so this list will reflect that statement.
If you want to, look into what proffesionals are using. Cymatics for example is okay being free, but I would never buy anything from them. Not worth it.

Instruments:

- Libraries - LABS by Spitfire; LANDR Chromatic; PianoBook; PluginBoutique
- Small Libraries - Vienna Symphonic Library; Mel-Lofi 2; Numa Player 2
- Vital
- Casette Drums
- Pianos - Winter Felt Piano; Claustrophobic Piano; Autumn Piano; The Felt Seiler; Softhammer; Dust Piano; Fallen Keys
- Karoryfer Cute Emily Guitar

Extra Notes: Whatever genre you're doing, you probably want Vital. Sites like presetshare have so many good presets for basically anything you could want it is incredible. Pads, synths, drums, keys (especially great, great keys), and more. Also might be the most premium sounding of the free stuff.

Plugins:

- Lofi - Vinyl; Origin Vintage; Cymatics Deja Vu
- Pitch Shifter - Formant
- Libraries - Cymatics
- Delay - Deelay
- Fire
- Reverb - Cymatics Space Lite
- Guitar Amp - Amplitube 5

Extra Notes: The Lofi stuff is primarily Lofi which is obvious, but it can be useful for other stuff. I've used Deja Vu as a pitch shifter. Fire also lets you adjust the width of a track which is good for mastering, as well as a lot of other stuff like a makeshift LFO.

DAWs:

- Cakewalk Sonar
- LMMS

Extra Notes: Sonar is far better than LMMS in every single way, however LMMS is more intuitive to use early on if you're unsure you have the discipline to learn the basics of a DAW in the beginning.

Sample Kits:

- Libraries - Clark Music; Cymatics; LANDR Chromatic

# Pixel Art

This is your Pixel Art Get-In started on the 15th of June 2026 by Stella-Lou Pique.

Software:

- Piskel (Web-Based)
- LibreSprite (Desktop)
- Resprite (Mobile)

Websites:

Colour Palettes - Lospec, Pixilart

Extra Notes: That's all you need on the equipment and resources side. Most of all you'll also need to learn about some rules of Dos-N-Don'ts of Pixel Art and some etiquette so you know where to start. Beginners should always start with 16x16 sprites and try imitating or making stuff similar to Terraria, Minecraft, Stardew Valley or things of that sort. There's a crap ton of examples to name in which you can look for inspiration, guidance, see how they use and evade traditional Pixel Art rules. You don't need to know much colour theory but remember a few key rules if you're choosing your own colours: If the colours has less light, they become darker but more saturated and closer to blue; if it has more light (such as a highlight) they become lighter but less saturated and closer to yellow. Pixel Art has a surprisingly low ceiling on where sprites start to look presentable, maybe even good. You can start doing spritework with no experience and with a couple weeks or months of experience you'll have something that wouldn't look off used as assets in the right game. Animations, dithering, anti-aliasing however are a different beast you'll get to at your own pace.
